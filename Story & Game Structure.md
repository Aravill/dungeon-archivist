# Types of games
## One-shots
- Generally a single session adventure
- Take anywhere between 1 to 8 hours to complete
- Negligible to no effects on the setting
	- A village may be saved, but the world remains the same place as it was before.
- Can serve as introductions to the game world and to test player chemistry
- Side-quests should be avoided; they eat up time without advancing the plot
## Short campaigns
- Spans several sessions, usually up to 5, of 1 to 8 hours
- Maintain plot continuity and have an overarching story
- Small effects on the setting
## Long campaigns
- More than 5 sessions of 1 to 8 hours
- Can easily be comprised of short campaigns
	- For example Stranger Things is a long campaign
	- But each season has its own, relatively self-contained story -  a short campaign
- Large, overarching story with plot continuity
- Character buildup
	- Having players explore NPC backgrounds and learn more about them
	- Evolving Player x NPC relationships
- Backstory resolution
	- Providing players with ways to tie up the loose ends of their own backstories 
- Large effects on the setting
# Making an adventure
## 3 Act Structure
- An adventure is like an episode of a TV show
	- Not a modern one, more like a show from the 2000s - procedural, like CSI Miami, The Mentalist, House or Stargate - the episodes are self contained
	- post Game of Thrones shows have a tendency of just being long movies cut up into episodes - there is a lot of continuity
- Acts can be nested
	- A campaign, with its own setup, confrontation and resolution, is made up of adventures, each with its own 3 acts 
### Acts
- Setup
	- The protagonists are introduced
	- The plot hook is thrown at them
- Confrontation
	- Plot thickens
	- Protagonists face challenges (encounters)
- Resolution
	- The protagonists lose or triumph
	- The plot concludes and is resolved with no loose ends
		- Except when the one-shot is an introduction to an adventure / campaign, then leave loose ends
### Example
- Stargate Atlantis: S01 E01 *Rising*
- Setup
	- The heroes meet at an expeditionary base in Icewind Dale
	- A portal was found that is believed to lead to an ancient city in another world
	- The players are paid to be mercenaries on the expedition
	- The city is believed to host lots of treasure and technology
	- The portal is summoned and heroes pass through
- Confrontation
	- Once on the other side, the portal breaks, leaving the expedition stuck
	-  The city exists, but is on the verge of destruction
	- The heroes must seek a way to save the city, city records speak of a powerful protection scroll nearby
	- The heroes venture for the scroll, but accidentally wake mysterious enemies - vampires - that they must battle
- Resolution
	- The enemies are revealed to be old enemies of the city's inhabitants
	- The big bad queen of the vampires is defeated
	- (In case of a one-shot)
		- The scroll is retrieved, but it can only turn on the portal
		- The heroes activate it and safely escape back to their world, leaving the city behind
	- (In case of a set-up for a longer adventure)
		- The scroll is retrieved and saves the city, leaving the heroes the ability to explore it
		- The portal is permanently closed, leaving the expedition stuck
## What makes up an Act
### Story Beats
- The important / cool things you want to happen
- Going back to the Stargate example, the beats would be:
	- The Grand Opening of the Portal
	- Awakening of the Ancient Vampire Spawn army
	- Defeating of the Vampire Queen
	- Salvation of the City
- They somewhat correspond to the 3 act structure
- Early story beats increase mystery & tension
- Mid-adventure beats play with mystery and maintain tension
- Ending beats resolve the story and tension and provide a reward
### Scenes
- Each scene must have
	- Its own backdrop
		- Location / Environment
		- Vibe
		- NPCs
	- A purpose
		- Moving the plot forward
		- It's valid to have a scene for the heroes to rest and interact, but then that is its purpose
- Avoid making scenes for the sake of having scenes
	- If nothing of interest is going to happen, don't make it a scene
	- If something of interest happens, it should be relevant to the plot
	- Of course you can screw with your players and create red herrings, but that should be the exception, not a common pattern
- Example from Stargate Atlantis: S01 E01 *Rising*
	- Airship flight scene at the very beginning of the adventure
	- Serves to introduce the paladin, Johan the Airship Pilot
	- Shows that he is a skilled pilot, doesn't listen to his superior's commands and is pretty confident in his abilities, leading to him saving the airship from a crash
	- Also shows he doesn't belong to the expedition and it's just an accident that he ends up joining it
- Scene count directly affects session length
	- Count heavily depends on how quickly you can progress through your story beats
	- For an adventure of 5 hours
		- 2 scenes for Setup; ~3 for Confrontation; 1 for Resolution
# Sandboxing vs Railroading
- Sandboxing = letting the players find their own purpose and fun
- Railroading = having a prepared story you want the players to experience
- Most DMs end up doing a mix of both - prepared story with specific beats, but let the players approach them in their own way, or potentially abandon them
- Sandboxing is hard to achieve and make fun
	- Preparation for every possibility is impossible
	- The players always want some kind of a story to experience, but also enjoy the sandboxing freedoms
	- A compromise must be reached
	- Feel free to say "I don't have that ready"
- Smoke and mirrors are your main DM weapons
	- Pretend like things you didn't plan were always planned by using cheat sheets and encounter tables
	- It's more important to have tools for improvisation, than to be ready for everything
- If players change their minds, change the backdrop of scenes, but keep the story beat
	- A vampire queen, in an evil castle, with a scroll of ancient knowledge => A crazy nomad shaman, in a desert, with a scroll of ancient knowledge
	- The players do not know what was planned, don't be afraid to swap out bits and pieces of the adventure while running it
